  • No trade has moved more than $100K against budget
  • Net trade-scope variance: +$6.5K, less than 0.1% of the trade base

Mitigants in Flight

  • Ramsgate GC contingency has absorbed −$93K of trade variance, doing what contingency is designed to do

  • Net buyout variance across the four reported projects is flat
Why this slide matters: Under a GMP, the GC absorbs subcontract pricing variance up to the cap. The unbought portion of each project is the live risk surface, where the difference between hitting and missing the GC margin sits. Closing out buyout cleanly on the in-flight projects is what protects the FY 2026 / FY 2027 GC net income shown in the SPC update.

Working Proposal · What's In, What's Out

A starting cut to react to. Anything in the keep column is what we think actually drives the conversation each cycle. Anything in the trim column comes back when it has news, not on a schedule.

Keep · Standing Set

The six things that earn their slot every cycle

  • Headline KPI tiles. Revenue, gross margin, EBITDA, cash, bonding capacity, backlog. Period-stamped, vs. budget, vs. prior period.
  • Active job one-liner. One row per project: status, % complete, GM, material change orders, schedule. The whole book on a single page.
  • Pipeline + backlog deltas. What was added, won, or lost since last cycle, with a forward-look on the next 60 to 90 days.
  • Risk register. Low / medium / high per active job, with a one-line note on what moved.
  • Bonding wedge. Capacity used and available, current cost spread, and the path to LV-direct bonding (this is the lever that ties to the SPC Extended BD plan).
  • CEO message. Short narrative that ties the numbers to what the team is actually doing. The story, not the metrics.
Trim · Revisit on Material Change

Comes back when it has news, not on a schedule

  • LV 2.0 strategic direction. Revisit annually or when a target moves materially, not every cycle.
  • Market & operating environment. Refresh when conditions shift (rates, ICE, AB 2011), otherwise summarized in the CEO message.
  • Prior-quarter commitments scorecard. Useful right after the period; lower-priority on standing cycles.
  • Detailed safety scorecard. One headline line on standing cycles; full deep-dive only on incident or annual review.
  • Org chart / staff allocation. Refresh when org changes, not standing.
  • Full P&L, balance sheet, cash flow statements. Stay in financials backup. The standing deck carries a one-page summary; the detail is available on request.

Between Quarters · Monthly Delivery

No second deck. The monthly delivery is the financial package, plus a small set of operating outputs that ride alongside it for visibility on what moved between quarters.

Monthly · Financials

The standing financial package

P&L vs. budget, balance sheet, and cash position. The same statements LV's controller already produces for IC and accounting, sent through unchanged. No reformatting, no narrative wrap.

Plus · Few Key Outputs

A short list of operating metrics alongside the financials

  • Backlog snapshot, with what was added or burned since last month
  • Active job statuses, with any project that flipped status
  • Bonding utilization, with any bond posted or released
  • Pipeline movement, with any new win, loss, or contracted award

Calibrate With Steyn · Four Open Questions

The cuts above are LV's working view. The cleaner this lands the better the standing reports get, so we'd rather walk through these in the room than guess.

Question 01 · Monthly Outputs

Are the four operating outputs above (backlog, job statuses, bonding utilization, pipeline movement) the right set to ride alongside the monthly financials? Add, drop, or swap until it is the smallest set Steyn would actually read each month.

Question 02 · Definitions

Where do we want shared definitions versus LV-internal ones? Backlog, pipeline, hit rate, and bonding utilization were all defined slightly differently in the Q1 walkthrough; one set of definitions makes every future cycle cleaner.

Question 03 · Threshold for Commentary

At what variance does a metric earn a written explanation versus just a number on a tile? Setting a clear bar (for example, ±10% vs. budget or prior period) keeps the standing deck from drowning in commentary on noise.

Question 04 · Format

Is a tiles-plus-light-commentary format the right ongoing shape, or would Steyn rather see a written narrative with the numbers in line? We can produce either; the one we don't pick disappears.

The sustainability test: every metric in the standing set has to be cheap enough to maintain that we'll still be running it in two years without a heroic effort. Anything that fails that test should not be in the standing set, even if it would be nice to have. The reductions above are a forcing function on that.
